⚡ The Energy System: How Food Becomes Fuel

These are the molecules, enzymes, and structures involved in converting the food you eat into ATP, the energy currency every cell in your body runs on.

ATP (Adenosine triphosphate) — The universal energy currency of the cell. Every process in your body, from thinking to moving to digesting, requires ATP. The entire goal of this protocol is to maximize how much ATP your mitochondria produce from the food you eat.

Mitochondria — Tiny organelles inside every cell that produce ATP through oxidative phosphorylation. Think of them as the engines. More mitochondria and better-functioning mitochondria mean more energy output, better body composition, and improved health. Chapters 2 and 9 focus heavily on these.

ETC (Electron Transport Chain) — A series of four protein complexes (I, II, III, IV) plus ATP synthase embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. Electrons from food pass along this chain, and the energy released is used to pump protons, creating the gradient that drives ATP production. This is where most of your energy is actually made.

Complex I–IV — The four enzyme complexes of the ETC. Complex I accepts electrons from NADH. Complex II from FADH₂. Complex III passes electrons via CoQ10. Complex IV transfers them to oxygen (producing water). Each pumps protons across the membrane, building the gradient that powers ATP synthase.

ATP Synthase (sometimes called Complex V) — A rotary molecular motor that literally spins as protons flow through it, catalyzing ADP + phosphate → ATP. Peter Mitchell won the Nobel Prize for figuring out how this works.

CoQ10 (Coenzyme Q10 / Ubiquinone / Ubiquinol) — An electron carrier that shuttles electrons between Complexes I/II and Complex III in the ETC. When you supplement CoQ10, you’re directly supporting this handoff step. Ubiquinol is the reduced (active) form with better absorption.

NADH and FADH₂ — Electron carriers produced during glycolysis, the TCA cycle, and β-oxidation. They deliver electrons to the ETC. More NADH/FADH₂ = more electrons = more proton pumping = more ATP. Think of them as delivery trucks bringing fuel to the power plant.

TCA Cycle (Tricarboxylic Acid Cycle / Krebs Cycle / Citric Acid Cycle) — A circular series of chemical reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that processes acetyl-CoA (from either glucose or fat) and produces NADH, FADH₂, and a small amount of ATP. It’s the common pathway where glucose and fat metabolism converge.

Acetyl-CoA (Acetyl Coenzyme A) — A two-carbon molecule that is THE convergence point of metabolism. Glucose produces it via PDH. Fatty acids produce it via β-oxidation. It enters the TCA cycle. When both glucose and fat are flooding the system, acetyl-CoA piles up and triggers the Randle Cycle. This molecule appears in nearly every chapter.

Oxidative Phosphorylation — The full process of using the ETC plus ATP synthase to produce ATP from NADH and FADH₂. This is where the big ATP payoff happens: roughly 30–32 ATP per glucose molecule (vs. only 2 from glycolysis alone). The protocol’s emphasis on mitochondrial health is about keeping this process running at maximum efficiency.

ROS (Reactive Oxygen Species) — Byproducts of normal ETC function, mainly superoxide (O₂⁻). Small amounts are normal and even useful for signaling. Excess amounts damage mitochondrial DNA, proteins, and membranes. PUFAs increase ROS production. Saturated fats produce less. This is why fat quality matters.

mtDNA (Mitochondrial DNA) — Mitochondria carry their own small DNA (16,500 base pairs, encoding 13 ETC proteins). Unlike nuclear DNA, mtDNA lacks protective histones and has poor repair mechanisms. When ROS damage mtDNA, the mitochondria produce defective ETC proteins, which produce more ROS, creating a vicious cycle.

🍬 Glucose Metabolism: From Carbs to Energy

These terms describe how your body processes carbohydrates, from the moment glucose enters your blood to when it becomes ATP or gets stored as glycogen.

Glucose — The simplest sugar and the primary fuel for most cells. Comes from digesting carbohydrates. Your brain runs almost exclusively on glucose. Blood glucose that stays too high is dangerous, which is why your body prioritizes clearing it.

Fructose — The sugar found in fruit alongside glucose. Unlike glucose, fructose goes primarily to the liver via GLUT5/GLUT2 transporters and doesn’t require insulin for uptake. It refills liver glycogen through an independent pathway. This is why fruit in the morning hits two fuel tanks at once (Chapter 5).

Glycolysis — The 10-step pathway that breaks glucose into two molecules of pyruvate, producing 2 ATP and 2 NADH. Happens in the cytoplasm, not in mitochondria. This is the only energy you get from glucose if your mitochondria aren’t working well.

PDH (Pyruvate Dehydrogenase) — THE critical gateway enzyme. Converts pyruvate (from glycolysis) into acetyl-CoA (for the TCA cycle). Requires vitamin B1 (thiamine) as a co-factor. Inhibited by high acetyl-CoA and NADH ratios, which is the core of the Randle Cycle. If PDH is blocked, glucose metabolism stalls and you produce lactate instead of ATP.

Glycogen — The storage form of glucose. Stored in muscles (~400g capacity) and liver (~100g). Muscle glycogen fuels exercise. Liver glycogen maintains blood sugar between meals. The protocol’s emphasis on morning fruit is partly about refilling depleted liver glycogen to support thyroid function.

GLUT4 (Glucose Transporter Type 4) — The protein door that lets glucose into muscle and fat cells. Normally hidden inside cells. Moves to the cell surface in response to two signals: insulin (via the PI3K/Akt cascade) and exercise (via AMPK/CaMKII). The more GLUT4 on the surface, the more glucose your cells absorb. Taurine and C3G both aim to increase GLUT4 activity.

Hexokinase — First enzyme of glycolysis. Traps glucose inside the cell by phosphorylating it to glucose-6-phosphate. Inhibited by its own product (G6P accumulation), which is the final step of the Randle Cycle’s glucose-blocking cascade.

PFK-1 (Phosphofructokinase-1) — The main rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis. Inhibited by cytoplasmic citrate (which accumulates during fatty acid oxidation). This is the middle step of the Randle Cycle.

DNL (De Novo Lipogenesis) — The process of converting glucose into fatty acids. Energetically expensive (wastes 23–25% of the energy). Rarely a significant source of fat storage under normal conditions. This is why the claim “excess carbs turn into fat” is biochemically misleading.

🔥 Fat Metabolism: From Dietary Fat to Energy or Storage

These terms describe how your body processes dietary fat, gets it into mitochondria, and either burns it or stores it.

Fatty Acids — The building blocks of dietary fat. Classified by chain length (short, medium, long) and saturation (saturated, monounsaturated, polyunsaturated). Different types burn differently in mitochondria and have different effects on membrane stability.

SFA (Saturated Fatty Acids) — Fats with no double bonds (e.g., stearic acid, palmitic acid). Chemically stable, resistant to oxidation. Burn cleanly in mitochondria with minimal ROS. The protocol’s “S-tier” fats.

PUFA (Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids) — Fats with two or more double bonds (e.g., linoleic acid from seed oils). Contain vulnerable bis-allylic hydrogens that break down easily in the high-heat, high-oxygen mitochondrial environment, producing toxic aldehydes like 4-HNE. The protocol’s “F-tier” fats (except omega-3s).

MCT (Medium-Chain Triglycerides) — Fats with 6–12 carbon chains (found in coconut oil). They bypass the CPT-1 shuttle entirely, diffusing directly into mitochondria for rapid oxidation. Almost impossible to store as body fat. This is why MCTs are the one fat allowed in the morning window.

CPT-1 (Carnitine Palmitoyltransferase 1) — The gatekeeper enzyme on the outer mitochondrial membrane. Long-chain fats must be conjugated to carnitine by CPT-1 to enter the mitochondria. Inhibited by malonyl-CoA (which rises when glucose is abundant). This is the molecular reason you don’t mix carbs and fats: high glucose → high malonyl-CoA → CPT-1 blocked → fat can’t be burned → fat gets stored.

L-Carnitine — The amino acid-like molecule that physically shuttles long-chain fatty acids across the inner mitochondrial membrane via the CPT system. Supplementing it increases the availability of this shuttle, potentially enhancing fat oxidation.

β-Oxidation (Beta-Oxidation) — The mitochondrial process that chops fatty acids into two-carbon units (acetyl-CoA), one at a time. Each round produces 1 NADH, 1 FADH₂, and 1 acetyl-CoA. This is how fat becomes energy.

Malonyl-CoA — A molecule produced from acetyl-CoA by the enzyme ACC (acetyl-CoA carboxylase). Its main role is blocking CPT-1, preventing fat from entering mitochondria. When glucose is high, malonyl-CoA rises — this is the reverse Randle Cycle in action.

4-HNE (4-Hydroxynonenal) — A toxic aldehyde produced when PUFAs undergo lipid peroxidation. Damages mitochondrial proteins and DNA. One of the main biochemical reasons the protocol avoids seed oils.

🔑 Insulin Signaling: The Master Nutrient Delivery System

These terms describe the cascade of events from insulin binding its receptor to glucose entering your cells — and what goes wrong in insulin resistance.

Insulin — A peptide hormone from pancreatic β-cells. Released when blood glucose rises. Its job is to open cell doors (GLUT4) so glucose can enter. Not a villain — insulin resistance (cells ignoring insulin’s signal) is the problem.

IR (Insulin Receptor) — A receptor on cell surfaces that insulin binds to, triggering the signaling cascade. It’s a tyrosine kinase: binding insulin makes it phosphorylate itself, starting the chain reaction.

IRS-1 (Insulin Receptor Substrate 1) — The first domino after the insulin receptor fires. When phosphorylated on tyrosine residues (good), it activates PI3K. When phosphorylated on serine residues (bad, caused by inflammatory signals), the cascade is blocked. This is the most common site of insulin resistance.

PI3K (Phosphoinositide 3-Kinase) — An enzyme activated by IRS-1 that converts PIP2 to PIP3 in the cell membrane. PIP3 then recruits Akt. Think of PI3K as the second domino in the insulin cascade.

Akt (Protein Kinase B) — The third domino. Activated by PI3K-generated PIP3. Akt phosphorylates AS160, releasing the brake on GLUT4 vesicles. Akt is also inactivated by ceramides (via PP2A), another route to insulin resistance.

AS160 (Akt Substrate of 160 kDa / TBC1D4) — A brake protein that normally keeps GLUT4 vesicles locked inside the cell. When Akt phosphorylates it, the brake releases, GLUT4 vesicles move to the surface, and glucose can enter.

HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance) — A blood test calculation (fasting insulin × fasting glucose ÷ 405) used to estimate insulin resistance. Lower is better. The taurine meta-analyses show significant HOMA-IR improvements.

DAGs (Diacylglycerols) — Lipid intermediates that accumulate inside muscle cells when fat intake exceeds oxidation capacity. They activate nPKCθ, which serine-phosphorylates IRS-1, blocking insulin signaling. This is the lipotoxicity model of insulin resistance (Shulman, 2014).

Ceramides — Another class of lipid intermediates (sphingolipids) that accumulate intracellularly. They activate PP2A, which directly dephosphorylates Akt, shutting down the insulin cascade at the third domino.

📡 Energy Sensing: How Cells Know When to Burn or Store

These molecules and enzymes act as fuel gauges and switches, telling your cells whether to burn fuel or store it.

AMPK (5’-AMP-Activated Protein Kinase) — Your cell’s master energy sensor. Activated when energy is low (high AMP:ATP ratio), as during exercise or fasting. Flips cells into “burn mode”: increases glucose uptake (via GLUT4), increases fat burning (by inhibiting ACC → lowering malonyl-CoA → freeing CPT-1), and stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis. C3G and black ginger both activate AMPK. Exercise is the most powerful AMPK activator.

PGC-1α (Peroxisome Proliferator-Activated Receptor Gamma Coactivator 1-Alpha) — The master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. When activated (by AMPK, exercise, cold exposure), it turns on the genes that build new mitochondria. PQQ supplementation targets PGC-1α. This is how you get MORE mitochondria, not just better ones.

ACC (Acetyl-CoA Carboxylase) — An enzyme that converts acetyl-CoA into malonyl-CoA. When ACC is active, malonyl-CoA rises and blocks fat burning (via CPT-1 inhibition). AMPK inhibits ACC, which is one of the main ways exercise promotes fat oxidation.

Leptin — A hormone produced by fat cells that signals energy sufficiency to the brain. More body fat = more leptin = brain understands energy stores are adequate. During aggressive dieting, leptin crashes, causing intense hunger and metabolic slowdown that can persist for over a year (Sumithran et al., 2011).

Ghrelin — The “hunger hormone,” produced mainly by stomach cells. Rises before meals, suppressed after eating. Also stimulates growth hormone release, which is part of why the protocol times fat intake in the evening (ghrelin → GH → overnight repair).

🧪 Hormones: The Chemical Messengers That Control Everything

Cortisol — A glucocorticoid produced in the adrenal glands. Follows a daily rhythm: peaks at waking (CAR), lowest at midnight. Acute cortisol is useful (mobilizes glucose, provides alertness). Chronic cortisol is destructive (promotes visceral fat storage via 11β-HSD1, breaks down muscle, suppresses thyroid). The protocol aims to keep cortisol in its natural rhythm by preventing blood sugar crashes.

CAR (Cortisol Awakening Response) — The 50–100% spike in cortisol within 30–60 minutes of waking. This is normal and adaptive. It primes your body for the day. The FTN protocol leverages this by pairing morning fruit with the natural cortisol peak to maximize glucose utilization.

11β-HSD1 (11-Beta Hydroxysteroid Dehydrogenase Type 1) — An enzyme that converts inactive cortisone into active cortisol within tissues. It’s more highly expressed in visceral (belly) fat than subcutaneous fat. This is why chronic stress specifically causes belly fat accumulation.

T3 (Triiodothyronine) — The ACTIVE thyroid hormone. Sets the pace of oxidative metabolism in every cell. Higher T3 = higher metabolic rate = more energy = warmer body temperature. The liver converts inactive T4 to T3 using deiodinase enzymes, which require glucose. This is Ray Peat’s core argument for why carbs support metabolism.

T4 (Thyroxine) — The INACTIVE thyroid hormone. Produced by the thyroid gland and converted to active T3 primarily in the liver. Low-carb diets can suppress T4→T3 conversion, reducing metabolic rate.

TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) — Produced by the pituitary gland to tell the thyroid to make more T4/T3. High TSH usually means the thyroid is underperforming (the brain is shouting louder to get the same output). A standard blood test marker.

GH (Growth Hormone) — Promotes fat oxidation, muscle repair, and cellular regeneration. Pulses most strongly during deep sleep. Stimulated by ghrelin. Evening fat intake provides the raw material (cholesterol) for hormones while GH drives overnight repair.

Testosterone — Primary androgen. Synthesized from cholesterol. Supports muscle mass, bone density, mood, and libido. Production peaks during sleep. The protocol’s evening fat meal provides cholesterol for testosterone synthesis during the overnight hormonal window.

⏰ Circadian Biology: Your Body’s Internal Clock

These are the genes and systems that create your body’s 24-hour rhythm, governing when nutrients are processed differently.

CLOCK (Circadian Locomotor Output Cycles Kaput) — A gene that, together with BMAL1, forms the positive arm of your molecular clock. CLOCK/BMAL1 proteins bind together and activate the transcription of thousands of genes on a ~24-hour cycle. This is the engine of your circadian rhythm at the cellular level.

BMAL1 (Brain and Muscle ARNT-Like 1) — CLOCK’s partner. Together they activate PER and CRY genes. When you see “CLOCK/BMAL1” in the text, think: the ON switch of the circadian cycle. Disrupting BMAL1 in mice causes severe metabolic disease, obesity, and shortened lifespan.

PER (Period genes (PER1, PER2, PER3)) — Proteins produced by CLOCK/BMAL1 activation. As PER proteins accumulate, they eventually inhibit CLOCK/BMAL1, creating the negative feedback loop. Think of PER as the OFF switch that creates the cycle. PER accumulates during the day and peaks in the evening.

CRY (Cryptochrome genes (CRY1, CRY2)) — Partners with PER to inhibit CLOCK/BMAL1. Together, PER/CRY form the negative arm of the clock. The cycle: CLOCK/BMAL1 turn on PER/CRY → PER/CRY accumulate → PER/CRY inhibit CLOCK/BMAL1 → PER/CRY degrade → CLOCK/BMAL1 restart. This takes ~24 hours.

SCN (Suprachiasmatic Nucleus) — A tiny region in the hypothalamus that serves as the “master clock.” Receives light information from the retina and synchronizes all peripheral clocks (liver, muscle, pancreas) to the light-dark cycle. Morning sunlight sets this clock, which is why the protocol starts the day with light exposure.

Chrononutrition — The study of how meal timing interacts with circadian rhythms to affect metabolism. The core finding: the same food produces different metabolic responses depending on WHEN you eat it. Morning insulin sensitivity is higher, DIT is greater, and glucose tolerance is better. This is the scientific basis for FTN and evening fat timing.

🦫 Gut and Microbiome

Resistant Starch (RS) — Starch that resists digestion in the small intestine and reaches the colon intact. Created by cooking then cooling starches (RS3 type). Fermented by gut bacteria into SCFAs. Lets you eat starch with a lower glucose response.

SCFAs (Short-Chain Fatty Acids) — Metabolites produced by gut bacterial fermentation of fiber and resistant starch. Primarily butyrate, propionate, and acetate. Butyrate feeds colon cells, reduces inflammation, and improves insulin sensitivity through the gut-liver-muscle axis.

TMAO (Trimethylamine N-Oxide) — A metabolite produced when gut bacteria process carnitine or choline. Associated with cardiovascular risk in observational studies (though causation is debated). Mitigated by aged garlic extract. Worth monitoring if supplementing L-carnitine long-term.

GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide 1) — A hormone released by intestinal L-cells that enhances insulin secretion and slows gastric emptying. Resistant starch fermentation stimulates GLP-1 release, which is one mechanism by which RS improves glucose tolerance.

🔬 Research Terms You’ll See in This Course

RCT (Randomized Controlled Trial) — The gold standard of clinical evidence. Participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control groups. When we say the evidence for taurine is “moderate RCT evidence,” we mean multiple RCTs exist but they’re small.

Meta-analysis — A statistical method that combines results from multiple studies to find overall patterns. The taurine data cited in Chapter 9 comes from meta-analyses pooling 5–9 RCTs.

N-of-1 trial — A formal experiment with one subject (you). Uses repeated crossover between treatment and control periods. The biohacker tracker in Chapter 10 is an informal version of this.

SMD (Standardized Mean Difference) — A statistical measure of effect size used in meta-analyses. An SMD of 0.2 is small, 0.5 is medium, 0.8 is large. The taurine HOMA-IR improvement (SMD −0.64) is a medium effect.

In vitro / In vivo — In vitro = in a test tube or cell culture. In vivo = in a living organism. When evidence is “in vitro only,” it means the effect was shown in cells but not yet confirmed in animals or humans. C3G evidence is largely in vitro.

Chapter 1: Your Body Is Not Broken, It’s Confused

Core Question: Why does your body store fat even when you’re trying to lose it?

Key Studies

Leibel, Rosenbaum & Hirsch (1995), New England Journal of Medicine — on metabolic adaptation to weight loss. | Sumithran et al. (2011), NEJM — on long-term hormonal changes after dieting. | Keys et al. (1950) — The Minnesota Starvation Experiment.

The Paranoid Squirrel (Upgraded)

Imagine a squirrel in autumn. It hoards acorns because winter is coming. Your body works the same way. It has survival software that’s been running for hundreds of thousands of years. When the environment signals scarcity, your body hoards energy as fat.

But here’s the key insight from modern endocrinology: this isn’t a vague instinct. It’s a coordinated hormonal defense system managed by a brain region called the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus monitors circulating hormones like leptin (from fat cells), ghrelin (from your stomach), insulin (from your pancreas), cortisol (from your adrenal glands), and thyroid hormones. It uses these signals to decide whether to speed up or slow down your metabolism.

When you aggressively cut calories, here’s what this system actually does, measured in peer-reviewed research:

What the Science Shows

Metabolic adaptation is real and measurable. Leibel and colleagues showed in their 1995 New England Journal of Medicine study that a 10% reduction in body weight produces a disproportionate 15% drop in total energy expenditure. Your body slows down MORE than the weight loss accounts for. This isn’t your imagination. It’s measurable in a lab.

Hunger hormones stay altered for over a year. Sumithran’s 2011 NEJM study tracked dieters for 12 months after losing weight. Their leptin (the “I’m full” hormone) was still 35% below baseline a year later. Their ghrelin (the “I’m hungry” hormone) stayed elevated. The hormonal drive to regain weight persisted long after the diet ended.

Thyroid output decreases. T3, the active thyroid hormone that sets the pace of your metabolism in every cell, drops during caloric restriction. Lower T3 means lower metabolic rate, lower body temperature, and less energy. Your body literally runs colder and slower.

Cortisol rises. Cortisol is your stress hormone. During dieting, it stays chronically elevated. Cortisol’s signature move is promoting fat storage, particularly around your midsection (visceral fat). This is the most dangerous type of fat because it wraps around your organs and releases inflammatory signals.

The Minnesota Starvation Experiment

The most dramatic demonstration of this came from Ancel Keys’ Minnesota Starvation Experiment (1944–1945). Thirty-six men were put on about 1,570 calories a day for six months. They experienced a 40% reduction in metabolic rate, far beyond what their weight loss would predict. They became obsessed with food, anxious, depressed, and socially withdrawn. When they started eating normally again, they regained fat rapidly, often overshooting their original body fat levels.

This illustrates the central problem. Aggressive calorie cutting doesn’t just make you lighter. It rewires your hormonal system to fight weight loss and accelerate regain. Any effective approach has to work WITH this biology, not against it.

The Metabolic Upregulation Approach

This protocol draws inspiration from Ray Peat, PhD (1936–2022), a biologist whose research on thyroid function and cellular energy production influenced the “pro-metabolic” movement. Peat’s core argument: when your cells produce energy efficiently (through healthy mitochondria), your hormones self-regulate, inflammation resolves, and body composition improves naturally.

Rather than restricting food, the protocol strategically increases the quality and timing of fuel to support mitochondrial function and thyroid output. The question shifts from “How do I eat less?” to “How do I burn more?” This isn’t a loophole in thermodynamics. It works by increasing the “calories out” side of the equation: higher resting metabolic rate, more spontaneous movement (called NEAT, or non-exercise activity thermogenesis), and more efficient nutrient processing.

Honest Limitations

A few things to keep in mind. The energy balance equation (calories in vs. calories out) is still real. Metabolic upregulation doesn’t override physics; it raises how many calories your body burns. Also, Ray Peat’s work was influential but largely published outside peer-reviewed journals. And individual responses vary hugely based on genetics, thyroid status, and metabolic starting point. This framework is a starting point, not a guarantee.

Chapter 2: The Engine Room — Mitochondria and Energy Production

Core Question: What are mitochondria, how do they turn food into energy, and why is their efficiency the single most important factor in body composition?

Key Studies

Mitchell (1961), Nobel Prize 1978 — chemiosmotic hypothesis. | Wallace (2005), PNAS — mitochondrial paradigm of disease. | Mootha et al. (2003), Nature Genetics — mitochondrial genes downregulated in diabetes. | Warburg (1924) — aerobic glycolysis in cancer cells.

What Mitochondria Actually Do

Every cell in your body contains hundreds to thousands of tiny structures called mitochondria. Their job is to convert the food you eat into ATP (adenosine triphosphate), which is the energy currency that powers everything: thinking, moving, digesting, repairing tissue, producing hormones.

Here’s the number that matters most in this course. When mitochondria are healthy, they extract roughly 30 to 32 ATP per glucose molecule through a process called oxidative phosphorylation. When they’re damaged or dysfunctional, cells fall back on a much less efficient process called glycolysis, which produces only 2 ATP per glucose. That’s a 15-fold difference in energy production from the same food.

This explains something that confuses a lot of people: why two individuals can eat the same meal and have completely different outcomes. One feels energized and stays lean. The other crashes, gets brain fog, and stores the calories as fat. The difference, in most cases, is mitochondrial function.

How Energy Production Works (The Relay Race)

Think of mitochondrial energy production as a relay race with four runners, called Complex I, II, III, and IV. These are protein structures embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

Food gets broken down into electron carriers called NADH and FADH₂ (see the Quick Reference Guide). These carriers hand their electrons to Complex I (NADH) or Complex II (FADH₂). The electrons then pass from runner to runner: Complex I → CoQ10 → Complex III → cytochrome c → Complex IV → oxygen (which combines with hydrogen to make water).

At each handoff, energy is released and used to pump hydrogen ions (protons) across the inner membrane. This creates a gradient, like water behind a dam. When the protons flow back through a molecular turbine called ATP synthase, the flow drives ATP production. Peter Mitchell won the 1978 Nobel Prize for figuring this out.

CoQ10, which appears in the supplement stack (Chapter 9), works right in the middle of this relay as the electron carrier between Complexes I/II and Complex III. Supplementing it is like greasing the baton handoff.

What Damages Mitochondria

Seed oils and PUFAs (polyunsaturated fats). The inner mitochondrial membrane is made of fatty acids. When unstable PUFAs from seed oils get incorporated into this membrane, they break down easily from the heat and reactive oxygen species (ROS) that energy production naturally generates. The breakdown products (especially one called 4-HNE) directly damage the mitochondrial machinery and its DNA. Saturated fats resist this damage because their chemical structure has no vulnerable weak points.

Physical inactivity. Your body builds new mitochondria in response to physical demand. A key study by Mootha et al. (2003) in Nature Genetics found that genes responsible for building mitochondria (specifically, genes controlled by a master switch called PGC-1α) are significantly downregulated in people with type 2 diabetes. Exercise is the strongest known activator of PGC-1α.

Nutrient deficiencies. The relay race requires specific co-factors: CoQ10 for the baton handoff, B vitamins (especially B1) for converting glucose into the right fuel, iron and copper at Complex IV. Without these, the chain bottlenecks and energy production drops.

Chronic inflammation and oxidative stress. Mitochondria have their own DNA (mtDNA), which is more vulnerable to damage than your regular DNA because it lacks protective packaging and has limited repair ability. Accumulated mtDNA damage produces defective machinery, which produces more ROS, which causes more damage. Douglas Wallace (2005) has argued this vicious cycle is a central driver of aging.

The Flywheel Effect

Better mitochondria create a positive feedback loop. More ATP means more energy, which makes you naturally more active. More activity builds more mitochondria (via PGC-1α). More mitochondria improve insulin sensitivity, meaning glucose goes to muscles instead of fat. Less body fat means less inflammation, which protects mitochondria from damage. Each improvement feeds the next one. This is compound interest for your metabolism.

The reverse is equally powerful: damaged mitochondria → low energy → inactivity → fewer mitochondria → worse glucose handling → more fat storage → more inflammation → more mitochondrial damage. The protocol’s entire purpose is to push you off this negative cycle and onto the positive one.

Limitations

The “mitochondria-first” theory of metabolic disease is compelling but not universally accepted. Some researchers (notably Gerald Shulman at Yale) argue that insulin resistance is caused primarily by fat buildup inside muscle cells, not mitochondrial dysfunction per se. The truth likely involves both: mitochondrial health and intracellular fat accumulation interact in complex ways.

Chapter 3: Fuel Types — Your Body Runs on Two Tanks

Core Question: What are the two main fuels your body burns, and how does each one reach your mitochondria?

Key Studies

Randle et al. (1963), Lancet — glucose-fatty acid cycle. | Hellerstein (1999) — de novo lipogenesis in humans. | Kelley & Mandarino (2000) — fuel selection in insulin resistance. | Hardie (2011) — AMPK as cellular energy sensor.

Two Fuels, One Destination

Your body has two primary fuel sources: glucose (from carbohydrates) and fatty acids (from fat). Both end up in the same place, your mitochondria, where they’re converted into ATP. But they take very different routes to get there. Understanding these routes is essential because the entire protocol is built around managing them.

The Glucose Pathway

When you eat carbs, your digestive system breaks them into glucose. Glucose enters your bloodstream and triggers insulin release from your pancreas. Insulin acts like a manager sending a signal to your cells: “open the doors.” The doors are GLUT4 transporters, proteins that move from inside cells to the surface, creating openings for glucose to enter.

This signaling cascade goes: insulin binds its receptor → activates a chain of enzymes (IRS-1 → PI3K → Akt → AS160) → GLUT4 moves to the cell surface. If any link in this chain is broken, that’s insulin resistance (see the Quick Reference Guide for each enzyme). Exercise activates GLUT4 through a completely different pathway (AMPK), which is why exercise improves glucose uptake even when the insulin pathway isn’t working well.

Once inside the cell, glucose goes through glycolysis (producing a small amount of ATP), then a key enzyme called PDH (pyruvate dehydrogenase) converts it to acetyl-CoA, which enters the TCA cycle and eventually the electron transport chain for the big ATP payoff. PDH requires vitamin B1 to function. This enzyme is also the main target of the Randle Cycle (Chapter 4).

The Fatty Acid Pathway

Dietary fat is broken into fatty acids during digestion. Unlike glucose, fatty acids don’t need insulin to enter cells. But they DO need a special shuttle system to enter mitochondria: the carnitine shuttle (CPT system).

A molecule called L-carnitine physically grabs fatty acid molecules and carries them across the inner mitochondrial membrane. Without enough L-carnitine, fats pile up outside the mitochondria with nowhere to go. Inside the mitochondria, fatty acids undergo β-oxidation, which chops them into two-carbon units (acetyl-CoA), each round producing energy carriers (NADH, FADH₂) that feed the electron transport chain.

The critical control point is an enzyme called CPT-1, which sits on the outer mitochondrial membrane and initiates the carnitine shuttle. CPT-1 is inhibited by a molecule called malonyl-CoA. When glucose is abundant, malonyl-CoA rises, and fat burning gets blocked. This is the molecular basis for why eating carbs and fat together causes problems (more in Chapter 4).

Why Your Body Prioritizes Glucose

When both glucose and fatty acids are present, your body burns glucose first. This isn’t random. Elevated blood glucose is genuinely dangerous (it damages blood vessels, nerves, and organs). Your body treats it as urgent: clear the glucose, worry about fat later. The fatty acids that get sidelined during glucose processing don’t disappear. They get stored.

Why Fat Makes You Fatter (Per Gram) Than Carbs

Dietary fat is stored as body fat at roughly 96 to 98% efficiency. It costs your body almost nothing to convert dietary fat into stored fat. Carbs, on the other hand, need to go through an expensive process called de novo lipogenesis (DNL) to become fat, which wastes about 23 to 25% of the energy. Hellerstein (1999) showed that under normal eating conditions, DNL contributes minimally to fat stores. Your body prefers to store excess carbs as glycogen (in muscles and liver) and burn them off as heat. Significant carb-to-fat conversion only happens during extreme, prolonged overfeeding.

This is why the protocol treats fat like a supplement: essential but requiring precision in type, amount, and timing.

AMPK: Your Cellular Burn Switch

AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) is an enzyme that senses when cellular energy is low. When ATP drops and AMP rises (like during exercise), AMPK activates and flips the cell into “burn mode”: increased glucose uptake, increased fat burning (by blocking malonyl-CoA production), and stimulation of new mitochondria production. David Hardie, who characterized AMPK, calls it a metabolic master switch. Several supplements in Chapter 9 (C3G, black ginger) target AMPK activation.

Chapter 4: The Traffic Jam — The Randle Cycle in Action

Core Question: What happens inside your cells when you eat high fat and high carbs at the same time?

Key Studies

Randle, Garland, Hales & Newsholme (1963), Lancet — the original glucose-fatty acid cycle paper. | Hue & Taegtmeyer (2009), Am J Physiol — “The Randle cycle revisited.” | Shulman (2014), Cell Metabolism — lipotoxicity model of insulin resistance.

The Original Discovery

In 1963, Philip Randle and colleagues published one of the most cited papers in metabolic biochemistry. Working with rat heart and muscle tissue, they discovered that glucose and fatty acids directly compete with each other for oxidation, without any hormonal mediation needed. They called this the “glucose-fatty acid cycle,” and it fundamentally changed how scientists understood fuel metabolism.

The key insight: when both fuels flood your system at once, they create a metabolic traffic jam. Neither burns efficiently, and the excess gets stored.

The Cascade: Step by Step

Step 1: Fatty acid breakdown raises acetyl-CoA levels

When fatty acids are being burned (β-oxidation), they produce lots of acetyl-CoA and NADH inside the mitochondria.

Step 2: Acetyl-CoA blocks glucose processing

The key enzyme that converts glucose-derived pyruvate into acetyl-CoA is called PDH (pyruvate dehydrogenase). PDH is inhibited by its own products: when acetyl-CoA and NADH are already high from fat burning, PDH slows down. Glucose metabolism stalls at the pyruvate stage.

Step 3: Backup causes a chain reaction

The TCA cycle gets backed up with excess intermediates, particularly citrate. Citrate leaks into the cytoplasm and inhibits PFK-1, the main rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis. This slows down the entire glucose pathway. Upstream, glucose-6-phosphate accumulates and inhibits hexokinase, the very first step. The cell effectively “refuses” glucose.

Step 4: Blood sugar stays high, insulin overcompensates

With cells refusing glucose, blood sugar stays elevated. Your pancreas pumps out more insulin to try to force glucose in. Now you have high insulin AND high blood sugar AND fatty acids floating around.

Step 5: Everything gets stored

Elevated insulin tells your fat cells to absorb fatty acids and stop releasing stored fat. The dietary fat from your meal gets stored. The glucose that can’t be processed efficiently gets rerouted. If this pattern repeats meal after meal, your cells gradually become less responsive to insulin (insulin resistance).

The Reverse: Glucose Blocks Fat Burning Too

The cycle works both ways. When glucose is abundant, a molecule called malonyl-CoA rises in the cytoplasm (produced by the enzyme ACC from glucose-derived citrate). Malonyl-CoA blocks CPT-1, the gatekeeper for fatty acid entry into mitochondria. Fat literally cannot get in to be burned. It gets rerouted to storage. This reverse mechanism was clarified later by McGarry and is sometimes called the “reverse Randle Cycle.”

Real Food Examples

  • Pizza: high-fat cheese on high-carb dough

  • Donuts: deep-fried (fat) sugary dough (carbs)

  • Pasta alfredo: cream and butter sauce on refined carbs

  • Ice cream: high-fat cream with high sugar

  • Cheeseburger with fries: fatty beef and cheese with starchy potatoes fried in seed oil

The most addictive, craveable foods on the planet are almost always high-fat AND high-carb. The food industry knows this combination overrides satiety signals.

The Solution: Separate Your Fuels

For carb-focused meals: keep fat under about 12 grams. Rice with shrimp. Potatoes with lean sirloin. Rice noodles with chicken breast.

For fat-focused meals: keep carbs under about 30 grams. Eggs in coconut oil with arugula. Ribeye with asparagus. Sardines with greens.

Each fuel gets its own window, burns cleanly, and doesn’t jam up the other.

The Contemporary Debate

The Randle Cycle is well-established in cardiac muscle and liver. In skeletal muscle, the picture is more complex. Shulman’s lab at Yale has argued that fatty acid-derived molecules (called DAGs and ceramides) directly impair insulin signaling, rather than the classical enzyme-inhibition cascade Randle described. Hue and Taegtmeyer’s 2009 review concluded that both mechanisms likely operate, possibly in different tissues and timeframes. For practical dietary purposes, the conclusion is the same: co-elevation of glucose and fatty acids impairs the clean metabolism of both.

Chapter 5: Chrononutrition — The Morning Advantage and Circadian Insulin Sensitivity

Core Question: How does the circadian clock regulate nutrient metabolism, and why does meal timing matter as much as meal composition?

Key Literature

Poggiogalle et al. (2018) “Circadian regulation of glucose, lipid, and energy metabolism in humans,” Metabolism. | Panda (2016) “Circadian physiology of metabolism,” Science. | Bo et al. (2015) “Is the timing of caloric intake associated with variation in diet-induced thermogenesis?” | Peat, R. on thyroid hormone, liver glycogen, and morning metabolism.

Your Body’s Internal Clock

Every cell in your body contains a molecular clock, a set of genes that cycle on roughly a 24-hour loop. The main players are CLOCK and BMAL1 (which turn genes on), and PER and CRY (which gradually accumulate and turn them off again, creating the cycle). You don’t need to memorize these names, but you’ll see them in research papers. The key point: a master clock in your brain (called the SCN, or suprachiasmatic nucleus) is set by morning light, and it synchronizes clocks in your liver, pancreas, muscles, and fat tissue.

This matters for eating because Poggiogalle et al. (2018) demonstrated that insulin sensitivity, glucose tolerance, and the thermic effect of food (how many calories you burn just digesting a meal) all follow this daily rhythm. Insulin sensitivity peaks in the morning and declines throughout the day. The thermic effect of food is roughly twice as high for morning meals compared to evening meals. The same food produces measurably different metabolic outcomes depending on when you eat it.

Morning Cortisol and the Glucose Window

The cortisol awakening response (CAR) represents a 50 to 100% surge in cortisol within 30 to 60 minutes of waking. In the context of the Fruit Till Noon protocol, this is significant because acute cortisol promotes hepatic glucose output (via gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis) while simultaneously priming peripheral tissues for glucose uptake. Combined with peak morning insulin sensitivity, this creates an optimal window for carbohydrate utilization.

Ray Peat emphasized that the liver requires glucose to convert inactive T4 (thyroxine) to active T3 (triiodothyronine) via the deiodinase enzymes. Overnight fasting depletes hepatic glycogen, and if glucose is not replenished by morning, the liver’s capacity for T4→T3 conversion diminishes. Cortisol rises further to compensate (mobilizing amino acids for gluconeogenesis), creating a catabolic, anti-metabolic hormonal environment. Peat’s prescription: replenish liver glycogen quickly with easily digestible sugars to support thyroid conversion and suppress excess cortisol.

The Dual Sugar Advantage: Glucose vs. Fructose

Fruit contains both glucose and fructose in roughly equal proportions. These two sugars follow different routes in your body. Glucose enters the general bloodstream and goes everywhere, triggering insulin and refilling muscle glycogen. Fructose takes a shortcut directly to your liver, where it refills liver glycogen without requiring much insulin at all.

This is why fruit is uniquely suited for the morning window: it replenishes two different fuel stores (muscle glycogen via glucose, liver glycogen via fructose) through two non-competing pathways simultaneously, without overloading either one. You’re filling two tanks at the same time through two separate hoses.

Why Fat in the Morning Impairs the Window

Adding dietary fat to the morning carbohydrate window activates the Randle Cycle mechanisms discussed in Chapter 4. Fatty acids from the meal elevate the acetyl-CoA/CoA ratio, inhibit PDH, and compete with glucose for oxidation. The clean insulin response that would otherwise drive glucose into cells efficiently becomes muddied. From a chrononutrition perspective, this is particularly wasteful because it squanders the period of peak insulin sensitivity.

The exception is medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs), specifically C8 (caprylic acid) and C10 (capric acid). These fatty acids bypass the CPT-1/carnitine shuttle system entirely because their chain length allows passive diffusion across the mitochondrial membrane. They are rapidly oxidized in the liver and converted to ketones or used directly for ATP production. MCTs therefore do not compete with glucose via the Randle mechanism in the same way long-chain fatty acids do.

Troubleshooting and Individual Variation

Circadian insulin sensitivity varies between individuals and is influenced by chronotype (morning larks vs. evening owls), shift work history, light exposure patterns, and existing metabolic status. People with significant insulin resistance may experience reactive hypoglycemia from fruit-only mornings, as their impaired GLUT4 response causes blood glucose to overshoot and then crash. The FTN Lite modifications (adding MCTs, collagen, or nonfat dairy) provide a buffer while mitochondrial and insulin-signaling adaptations develop.

Limitations

Most chrononutrition research is observational or uses acute feeding studies. Long-term RCTs comparing morning carbohydrate-loaded diets with isocaloric evening-loaded diets are scarce and show mixed results. The mechanistic rationale is strong, but the magnitude of benefit from meal timing versus total dietary quality remains debated.

Chapter 6: Lipid Biochemistry — The Fat Hierarchy and Evening Metabolism

Core Question: How do different fatty acid classes affect mitochondrial function, and why does the circadian system favor fat metabolism in the evening?

Key Literature

Peat, R. on PUFA, oxidative stress, and thyroid suppression. | Lands (2012) “Consequences of essential fatty acids,” Nutrients. | St-Onge & Jones (2002) “Physiological effects of medium-chain triglycerides,” J Nutr. | Van Cauter et al. (1991) on nocturnal GH pulsatility.

Fatty Acid Chemistry: Why the Type of Fat Matters

Fatty acids come in different shapes. Saturated fats have straight, stable carbon chains with no weak points. Polyunsaturated fats (PUFAs) have multiple kinks (double bonds) in their chains, and these kinks create chemically vulnerable spots that break down easily when exposed to heat and oxygen, exactly the conditions inside your mitochondria.

When PUFAs get incorporated into your mitochondrial membranes (which they readily do), they break down from the ROS that energy production naturally generates. The breakdown products, especially a reactive molecule called 4-HNE, directly damage the electron transport chain and mitochondrial DNA. This is the biochemical basis for avoiding seed oils. Saturated fats resist this damage because they have no vulnerable double bonds.

Ray Peat spent decades arguing that the modern dietary shift from saturated fats to industrial seed oils (soybean, canola, corn, sunflower) has fundamentally compromised human mitochondrial function. While this remains debated in mainstream nutrition science, the chemistry of lipid peroxidation is well-established.

The Fat Hierarchy: Biochemical Rationale

S-Tier — MCTs and Stearic Acid: MCTs bypass CPT-1 entirely, providing rapid hepatic energy and ketone production (St-Onge & Jones, 2002). Their storage as body fat is minimal because they are preferentially oxidized. Stearic acid (C18:0) has a unique metabolic profile: it is converted to oleic acid (C18:1) by stearoyl-CoA desaturase (SCD-1) as needed, and evidence suggests it increases mitochondrial fusion (enhancing ETC efficiency) through a mechanism involving the mitofusin proteins.

A-Tier — EPA/DHA: These long-chain omega-3 PUFAs are the exception to the anti-PUFA principle. They serve as precursors to resolvins and protectins (specialized pro-resolving mediators) that actively terminate inflammatory cascades. They also modulate membrane fluidity in neural tissue. However, they are still susceptible to peroxidation, which is why whole-food sources (fresh fish, shellfish) are preferred over concentrated fish oil supplements, where oxidation during processing and storage is common.

B-Tier — Monounsaturated fats (oleic acid): A single double bond makes MUFAs more susceptible to peroxidation than SFAs but far less than PUFAs. Oleic acid (C18:1) is the predominant fatty acid in olive oil and is a precursor for hormonal synthesis. Strategic, sparse use.

F-Tier — Industrial seed oils and trans fats: Linoleic acid from soybean, canola, corn, and sunflower oils. Trans fats from partial hydrogenation. Both damage mitochondrial membranes, impair ETC function, and promote inflammatory signaling via prostaglandin E2 and thromboxane A2 pathways.

Evening Fat Timing: Circadian Rationale

Van Cauter et al. (1991) demonstrated that growth hormone (GH) secretion follows a robust circadian pattern, with the largest pulse occurring during the first period of slow-wave sleep. GH is a potent lipolytic hormone that promotes fat oxidation. Ghrelin, the orexigenic peptide that stimulates GH release, is also elevated in the evening. Evening fat consumption provides the substrate (cholesterol, SFAs) for overnight steroidogenesis (testosterone, progesterone, cortisol precursors) while aligning with the natural shift toward fat oxidation.

Additionally, evening insulin levels are naturally lower if the protocol has been followed throughout the day (carbs consumed earlier, insulin has returned to baseline). Lower insulin means reduced lipogenic signaling and improved fat oxidation. Consuming fat when insulin is low allows it to be processed without the storage-promoting effects of elevated insulin.

Chapter 7: Glycogen Dynamics — Starches, Training, and Resistant Starch

Exercise-Induced GLUT4 Translocation

Richter and Hargreaves (2013) established that contraction-stimulated glucose uptake in skeletal muscle operates through an AMPK/CaMKII-dependent pathway that is entirely independent of insulin signaling. During exercise, intracellular calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum activates CaMKII, while ATP depletion activates AMPK. Both converge to stimulate GLUT4 translocation to the plasma membrane. This creates a “protected window” where glucose can enter muscle cells regardless of insulin resistance status.

Post-exercise, insulin sensitivity in the trained muscle is elevated for 24 to 48 hours. Ivy and Kuo (1998) showed that glycogen synthase activity and GLUT4 protein abundance are both upregulated following glycogen-depleting exercise, creating an environment where ingested carbohydrates are preferentially directed toward muscle glycogen resynthesis rather than fat storage. The magnitude of this effect is proportional to the degree of glycogen depletion: the more glycogen you burn during training, the wider the post-exercise nutrient partitioning window.

Resistant Starch: Microbiome-Metabolic Axis

Resistant starch (RS) was classified by Englyst and colleagues into four types. RS2 (raw granular starch) and RS3 (retrograded starch, formed by cooking then cooling) are most relevant here. RS3 formation occurs when amylose chains reassociate during cooling into crystalline structures that resist α-amylase hydrolysis in the small intestine.

In the large intestine, RS is fermented by saccharolytic bacteria (primarily Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia intestinalis, and Eubacterium rectale) to produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), predominantly butyrate. Butyrate is the primary fuel for colonocytes and has systemic effects including: enhanced intestinal barrier integrity, suppression of NF-κB inflammatory signaling, improved hepatic insulin sensitivity via FFAR2/FFAR3 (free fatty acid receptor) activation, and increased GLP-1 secretion from enteroendocrine L-cells (Topping & Clifton, 2001).

From the protocol’s perspective, RS provides the carbohydrate benefits (glycogen replenishment for the starch that IS digested) while simultaneously feeding butyrate-producing bacteria, improving insulin sensitivity through a gut-liver-muscle axis distinct from the direct metabolic pathways discussed in earlier chapters.

Practical Implementation

Cook rice or potatoes, refrigerate for 12 to 24 hours, then gently reheat. Studies show that RS3 content increases with each cook-cool cycle (up to a point). Start slowly: RS can cause significant gas and bloating in individuals with dysbiotic microbiomes because fermentation produces CO₂ and H₂ alongside SCFAs. Gradual introduction allows the microbiome to adapt.

Starch scaling: highest starch intake post-workout (when GLUT4 is maximally expressed and glycogen synthase is activated), moderate starch pre-workout (to top off glycogen), minimal starch at rest (when insulin sensitivity is lower and glycogen stores are full).

Chapter 8: Insulin Physiology — Beyond the Villain Narrative

Core Question: How does insulin signaling actually work, what causes insulin resistance at the molecular level, and how does each protocol rule target a specific node in this system?

Key Literature

Shulman (2014) “Ectopic lipid in the pathophysiology of insulin resistance,” Cell Metabolism. | DeFronzo (2004) “Pathogenesis of type 2 diabetes mellitus.” | Petersen & Shulman (2018) “Mechanisms of insulin action and insulin resistance.” Physiol Rev.

The Insulin Signaling Cascade

Insulin is released by your pancreas when blood glucose rises. It binds to a receptor on your cell’s surface and triggers a chain reaction of enzyme activations: Insulin receptor → IRS-1 → PI3K → Akt → AS160 → GLUT4 moves to the cell surface → glucose enters. (See the Quick Reference Guide for what each abbreviation means.)

The whole cascade takes about 5 to 10 minutes. The critical point: if ANY link in this chain is disrupted, glucose can’t get into cells efficiently. The most common break point is IRS-1, which is why it comes up repeatedly.

What Causes Insulin Resistance at the Molecular Level

Insulin resistance isn’t your cells suddenly deciding to ignore insulin. It’s a specific molecular disruption. The most well-supported model comes from Gerald Shulman’s lab at Yale (Petersen & Shulman, 2018):

When you eat more fat than your cells can burn, fat-derived molecules called DAGs (diacylglycerols) accumulate inside muscle cells. DAGs activate an enzyme called PKCθ, which modifies IRS-1 in a way that blocks it from passing the insulin signal forward. Think of it like putting a lock on the second domino in a domino chain. Insulin can knock over the first domino all it wants, but the signal stops at IRS-1.

A second pathway involves molecules called ceramides, which directly shut down Akt (the third domino). Between DAGs hitting IRS-1 and ceramides hitting Akt, the insulin cascade gets attacked from multiple points.

How Each Protocol Rule Targets Insulin Sensitivity

  • FTN leverages peak morning circadian insulin sensitivity (fewer GLUT4 translocation events needed per unit glucose).

  • Fuel separation prevents DAG/ceramide accumulation by avoiding chronic fatty acid oversupply during glucose-processing periods.

  • Pre-lunch movement activates AMPK-mediated GLUT4 translocation (insulin-independent glucose clearance).

  • Taurine upregulates PI3K/Akt/GLUT4 via the pathway documented in multiple rodent and cell-culture studies (Zhang et al., 2021).

  • L-carnitine enhances CPT-1 mediated fatty acid clearance, reducing intracellular lipid accumulation that drives serine kinase activation.

  • PUFA avoidance reduces the supply of lipid peroxidation products (4-HNE) that activate JNK and IKKβ inflammatory kinases, which also serine-phosphorylate IRS-1.

Limitations

The insulin-centric model of obesity (“carbohydrate-insulin model” proposed by David Ludwig and others) remains contested. Large-scale metabolic ward studies (Hall et al., 2015) found that isocaloric low-fat diets produced equal or greater fat loss than low-carb diets, challenging the idea that insulin per se drives obesity. The reality is likely more nuanced: insulin is a key variable but not the sole determinant. Total energy balance, satiety, dietary quality, and individual genetic variation all matter.

Evidence-Based Framework

Supplements in this protocol are not wellness products. Each targets a specific enzymatic bottleneck identified in previous chapters. The evidence for each ranges from robust RCTs to mechanistic animal data with limited human confirmation. Intellectual honesty requires distinguishing between these levels of evidence. For each supplement, we’ll specify: the molecular target, the proposed mechanism, the quality of evidence, and acknowledged limitations.

Taurine (1–4g/day)

Target: PI3K/Akt/GLUT4 pathway; mitochondrial membrane stability; oxidative stress.

Mechanism: Zhang et al. (2021) demonstrated in HepG2 cells and diabetic rats that taurine upregulates expression of PI3K, Akt, and GLUT4, improving insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. A 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (N=209) found that taurine significantly reduced HbA1c (SMD −0.41), fasting blood sugar (SMD −1.28), and HOMA-IR (SMD −0.64). A larger 2024 meta-analysis of 9 RCTs in overweight/obese adults found that 3g/day improved glycemic control, with HbA1c reduction of 0.37% and HOMA-IR improvement, particularly in obese participants.

Limitations: Most human RCTs are small (N<100), short-term (8–12 weeks), and conducted in diabetic or obese populations. Extrapolation to healthy, lean individuals seeking metabolic optimization is speculative. Mechanism of action in humans is inferred from animal models.

Vitamin B1 / Thiamine (100–300mg/day)

Target: Pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH), α-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, transketolase.

Mechanism: Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) is an essential co-factor for PDH, the enzyme that converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA for TCA cycle entry. Without adequate B1, glucose metabolism stalls at pyruvate, producing lactate instead of acetyl-CoA. This mimics the Warburg effect at a nutrient-deficiency level. Lonsdale (2006) documented that subclinical B1 deficiency is widespread due to sugar, caffeine, and alcohol consumption, all of which deplete B1. High-dose benfotiamine (a lipid-soluble B1 form with improved bioavailability) has shown benefit in diabetic neuropathy RCTs.

Limitations: Evidence for B1 supplementation improving metabolic rate in non-deficient individuals is minimal. The rationale is strongest for preventing deficiency-induced metabolic impairment rather than achieving supraphysiological enhancement.

L-Carnitine (1–3g/day)

Target: CPT-1/carnitine shuttle; GLUT4 expression.

Mechanism: L-carnitine is the obligate shuttle for long-chain fatty acid transport across the inner mitochondrial membrane via the CPT system. Brass (2000) reviewed evidence that carnitine supplementation enhances fat oxidation during exercise, particularly at higher doses. Additional evidence suggests carnitine upregulates GLUT4 expression in muscle and increases androgen receptor density. Oral bioavailability is approximately 14–18%, which is why injectable forms (used in clinical settings) are more potent.

Limitations: Carnitine metabolism by gut bacteria produces TMAO (trimethylamine N-oxide), which has been associated with cardiovascular risk in some observational studies (though causality is debated). Aged garlic extract may mitigate this by altering gut microbial composition. The GLUT4 and androgen receptor data are primarily from animal models.

Advanced Stack: C3G, Black Ginger, CoQ10, PQQ

C3G (cyanidin-3-glucoside): An anthocyanin that activates AMPK and enhances GLUT4 translocation in adipocytes. Evidence is primarily from in vitro and rodent studies. Human RCTs are lacking. Proposed mechanism: AMPK activation → ACC inhibition → improved fatty acid oxidation + direct GLUT4 upregulation in muscle.

Black ginger (Kaempferia parviflora): Contains polymethoxyflavones, particularly 5,7-dimethoxyflavone (DMF). Thai traditional medicine and limited clinical data suggest thermogenic, PDE5-inhibitory (vasodilatory), and AMPK-activating effects. Evidence quality is low; most studies are small Thai clinical trials or rodent models.

CoQ10 (ubiquinone/ubiquinol): An essential electron carrier between Complexes I/II and III in the ETC. Endogenous synthesis declines with age. Evidence for cardiovascular benefit is moderate (Q-SYMBIO trial showed reduced cardiovascular mortality). Metabolic optimization evidence in healthy individuals is limited.

PQQ (pyrroloquinoline quinone): Activates PGC-1α, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. Evidence in rodent models is compelling for mitochondrial density increases. Human data is extremely limited (small studies showing improved sleep quality and reduced CRP).

Critical Evaluation

The evidence hierarchy for this supplement stack is: Taurine (moderate human RCT evidence for metabolic endpoints) > B1 (strong biochemical rationale, deficiency well-documented, supplementation data mainly in clinical populations) > L-Carnitine (moderate evidence for fat oxidation, GLUT4 data is animal-model) > CoQ10 (moderate cardiovascular evidence, metabolic evidence limited) > C3G and PQQ (primarily pre-clinical) > Black Ginger (very limited evidence). Users should calibrate their expectations accordingly.

Chapter 10: N-of-1 Methodology — Self-Experimentation as Applied Science

Core Question: How do you apply the scientific method to your own biology, and what distinguishes rigorous self-tracking from confirmation bias?

Key Literature

Lillie et al. (2011) “The n-of-1 clinical trial: the ultimate strategy for individualizing medicine?” | Roberts (2004) “Self-experimentation as a source of new ideas.” | Dallal & Gaines (1998) on single-subject research design.

The N-of-1 Trial

An N-of-1 trial is a formal clinical trial design in which a single patient undergoes repeated crossover periods of active treatment and control (or placebo). Lillie et al. (2011) argued in the Personalized Medicine journal that N-of-1 trials are the gold standard for individualized treatment decisions because they control for the largest source of variance in medicine: inter-individual differences. Population-level RCTs tell you what works on average; N-of-1 trials tell you what works for you.

The biohacker tracker described in this protocol is an informal N-of-1 design. To elevate it from casual journaling to meaningful data, several methodological principles must be applied:

Methodological Rigor

Baseline period: Minimum 5 to 7 days of tracking before any intervention. This establishes your personal variance. Without it, you cannot distinguish signal from noise.

Single-variable manipulation: Change exactly one thing at a time. Run it for a minimum of 7 days (ideally 14). This is the only way to attribute causation.

Washout periods: When switching supplements or dietary patterns, allow 3 to 5 days of washout to clear residual effects.

Blinding limitations: True blinding is impossible in self-experimentation. Acknowledge that expectation effects (placebo) will inflate perceived benefits. Look for objective markers (body temperature, sleep metrics, workout performance numbers) in addition to subjective ratings.

Confirmation bias: The biggest threat to self-experimentation. You want the intervention to work, so you unconsciously interpret ambiguous data favorably. Countermeasure: pre-register your hypothesis and success criteria before starting. Write down: “I predict X will improve by Y within Z timeframe.” Then evaluate honestly.

Key Biomarkers to Track

  • Waking oral temperature (proxy for metabolic rate / thyroid function)

  • Resting heart rate (higher RHR can indicate higher metabolic rate, within healthy ranges)

  • Sleep quality metrics (via wearable or subjective 1–10 scale)

  • Training performance (specific lifts, reps, RPE)

  • Subjective energy, mood, focus (1–10 scales, AM and PM)

  • Cravings frequency and intensity

  • Body composition changes (waist circumference, photos, weight trends)

Blood work (quarterly): fasting glucose, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, lipid panel, thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4), testosterone (total and free), CRP.

Chapter 12: Nutrient Variety — The Side Quest That Changes Everything

Core Question: Why does eating the same “healthy” foods every day eventually work against you?

The Problem with Monotony

Once you have your daily structure locked in, an insidious trap awaits: eating the same meals every day. Bodybuilders are notorious for this. Chicken, rice, and broccoli on repeat for months. It feels disciplined. It’s actually counterproductive.

When you feed your body identical inputs repeatedly, several things happen. Your gut begins producing only the enzymes needed for those specific foods and stops making others. Your thyroid function downregulates because the system recognizes it doesn’t need peak output to handle predictable inputs. Your mitochondrial efficiency increases, which sounds good but actually means you burn fewer calories processing the same food. Your gut microbiome diversity collapses because different bacteria need different foods, and the strains that don’t get fed die off.

Humans are seasonal eaters by design. For hundreds of thousands of years, our diets changed dramatically with the seasons. Spring foods differed from summer, fall, and winter. This constant rotation kept our metabolism guessing, our gut diverse, and our nutrient coverage comprehensive.

Main Quest vs Side Quest

Think of your nutrition in two categories. Main quest is your macros: the protein, carbs, and fats that form your daily foundation. Side quest is your micros: the vitamins, minerals, enzymes, flavonoids, and phytochemicals that come from variety.

Your main quest can and should be consistent. Have your anchor proteins, your go-to carbs, your cooking fats. But within that consistency, rotate. Don’t eat chicken every day. Cycle between beef, bison, fish, shrimp, and lamb. Don’t eat only white rice. Rotate with potatoes, sweet potatoes, sourdough, and oats. Don’t cook only with coconut oil. Occasionally use butter, olive oil, or tallow.

Protein Rotation

Each protein source has unique amino acid ratios, fat profiles, and micronutrients. Ground beef differs from flank steak. Beef differs from bison. Red meat differs from seafood. Even within a single category like beef, rotating between cuts gives you different nutrient profiles.

Build a roster: ground beef, chuck roast, flank steak, bison, lamb, shrimp, scallops, white fish, and chicken. Buy 3 to 4 sources weekly and rotate daily. You don’t need to be rigid. Just don’t eat the exact same protein every meal for weeks on end.

The Accoutrement Roster

This is where the real nutritional edge lies. Herbs, spices, and specific vegetables contain compounds that no macro-focused tracking app will ever account for. These are your micronutrient side quests:

  • Garlic provides allicin, which supports glutathione production, your body’s master antioxidant.

  • Ginger contains gingerols and shogaols that act as digestive enzymes and anti-inflammatories.

  • Rosemary offers mitochondrial protection and supports insulin sensitivity.

  • Cayenne has a direct thermogenic effect, increasing calorie burn.

  • Parsley supports estrogen metabolism through aromatase inhibition.

  • Seaweed provides iodine and trace minerals that are nearly impossible to get from land-based foods.

  • Red onions are loaded with quercetin, a powerful anti-inflammatory flavonoid.

Get in the habit of adding herbs and vegetables to every meal. A handful of arugula here. A sprinkle of rosemary there. Ginger grated into a stir-fry. These small additions compound over weeks and months into significant nutritional advantages.

Cycling Culinary Styles

An enjoyable way to implement variety is cycling entire culinary traditions. Spend a few weeks cooking Italian-inspired meals (olive oil, basil, tomatoes, oregano). Then switch to Greek (lemon, dill, feta, olives). Then Southeast Asian (ginger, lemongrass, fish sauce, lime). Then Mexican (cilantro, cumin, lime, chili).

Each tradition uses different herbs, spices, cooking methods, and ingredient combinations. Cycling between them naturally rotates your micronutrient intake without requiring a spreadsheet.

Chapter 13: Phases — Activation, Stabilization, and Living Your Life

Core Question: How long do you run this protocol, and what happens when you stop?

The Protocol Is Not Forever

If a protocol requires perfection forever, it has failed. Chapter 14: Troubleshooting — When Things Don’t Click

Core Question: What do you do when you hit a problem?

Every Problem Has a Specific Fix

The protocol is a system. When something goes wrong, it’s almost always traceable to a specific cause. Here are the most common problems and their solutions.

Problem: Energy Crashes in the Morning

You’re not eating enough fruit. This is the most common issue. People start FTN and eat a handful of berries, then wonder why they’re crashing by 10 AM. Your body needs fuel. Eat more fruit. If that doesn’t help, add MCT oil to a smoothie. If that still doesn’t help, transition to FTN Lite with collagen and nonfat dairy.

Also check: are you getting morning sunlight? Sunlight triggers the cortisol response that supports morning energy. Without it, you’re missing a key signal.

Problem: Gaining Fat on the Protocol

This usually means one of five things:

  • You’re mixing fats and carbs. Even small amounts of hidden fat in a carb meal trigger the Randle Cycle. Check ingredient labels. Cook your own food.

  • You’re eating too much before your metabolism has caught up. The protocol is about signaling abundance, but if your mitochondria are still weak, flooding them with fuel they can’t process leads to storage. Scale back portions.

  • You’re too sedentary. The protocol assumes you’re moving. Without regular physical activity, the metabolic signaling doesn’t work as intended. Add steps, NEAT, and intensity.

  • You’re consuming hidden PUFAs. Restaurant food is almost always cooked in seed oils. You may think you’re eating clean, but if you’re eating out frequently, you’re consuming inflammatory fats.

  • Your mitochondria aren’t ready for high carb intake. If you still crash after meals and have brain fog, your engine can’t handle the fuel yet. Focus on the supplement stack (taurine, B1, ALCAR, CoQ10, PQQ) and reduce carb volume until your mitochondria improve.

Problem: Losing Weight Too Fast

If you’re losing more than you want, you need more fuel. Add starches to your pre and post-workout meals. Consider FTN Lite with added protein. Increase your overall calorie intake, keeping the fuel separation principle intact. You can also reduce FTN to 3 days per week and eat normal balanced breakfasts on other days.

Problem: Training Early in the Morning

The protocol assumes midday or afternoon training, but many people train early. Adaptations:

  • The night before: have a high-calorie meal a few hours before bed with starchy carbs, protein, and moderate fat. This pre-loads glycogen.

  • Pre-workout: consume easily digestible carbs like honey, dates, or pomegranate juice. Take your supplement stack. Have caffeine if desired.

  • Post-workout: continue eating fruit for an hour or two. If needed, push your first solid meal window up to 10:30 or 11 AM.

Problem: Alcohol

Alcohol is metabolically disruptive, but total abstinence isn’t required. The protocol was developed while the creator was drinking socially several times per week. The key rules: frontload your calories for that day. Stop eating one hour before drinking. Absolutely no junk food while drinking or afterward. Alcohol isn’t what makes you fat. The garbage food you eat while metabolically compromised from alcohol is what makes you fat.

Problem: Social Pressure and Practicality

You will encounter situations where the protocol isn’t practical: family dinners, work events, travel. This is normal. One off-protocol meal does not undo weeks of metabolic adaptation. Enjoy the meal, return to the protocol the next day. If you’re in the Ongoing Pulse phase, this is literally built into the system as your freedom day.

What to Avoid at Trader Joe’s

Trader Joe’s is generally good, but there are traps. The protocol’s rules still apply:

  • Avoid anything fried or breaded in the frozen section (coconut shrimp, orange chicken, potstickers). These are deep-fried in seed oils.

Glossary of Key Terms

Acetyl-CoA: A two-carbon molecule that is the universal entry point into the TCA cycle. Produced from both glucose (via PDH) and fatty acids (via β-oxidation).

Adaptive thermogenesis: A disproportionate reduction in metabolic rate during caloric restriction that exceeds what reduced body mass alone would predict.

AMPK: 5’-AMP-activated protein kinase. A cellular energy sensor activated by rising AMP:ATP ratio that shifts cells from anabolic to catabolic metabolism.

AS160 / TBC1D4: Akt substrate of 160 kDa. A Rab-GTPase-activating protein that, when phosphorylated by Akt, releases GLUT4 vesicles for membrane translocation.

β-oxidation: The mitochondrial pathway that sequentially cleaves two-carbon units from fatty acyl-CoA, producing acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH₂.

Bis-allylic hydrogen: A hydrogen atom located on a carbon between two double bonds in a PUFA. Exceptionally vulnerable to radical abstraction, initiating lipid peroxidation.

Butyrate: A short-chain fatty acid produced by colonic fermentation of resistant starch. Primary fuel for colonocytes; anti-inflammatory systemically.

CAR: Cortisol awakening response. A 50–100% surge in cortisol within 30–60 min of waking.

Ceramides: Sphingolipid intermediates that activate PP2A, directly dephosphorylating and inactivating Akt to impair insulin signaling.

CPT-1: Carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1. Rate-limiting enzyme for long-chain fatty acid entry into mitochondria. Inhibited by malonyl-CoA.

DAGs: Diacylglycerols. Lipid intermediates that activate nPKCθ/ε, leading to serine phosphorylation of IRS-1 and insulin resistance.

De novo lipogenesis (DNL): The metabolic pathway converting non-lipid precursors (primarily glucose) into fatty acids. Energetically expensive (~23–25% loss).

ETC: Electron transport chain. Complexes I–IV plus ATP synthase in the inner mitochondrial membrane.

4-HNE: 4-Hydroxynonenal. A reactive aldehyde produced from PUFA peroxidation that covalently damages proteins and DNA.

GLUT4: Glucose transporter type 4. The insulin- and contraction-responsive glucose transporter in muscle and adipose tissue.

IRS-1: Insulin receptor substrate 1. Key adaptor protein in insulin signaling. Tyrosine phosphorylation activates; serine phosphorylation inhibits.

Malonyl-CoA: Cytoplasmic intermediate produced by ACC from acetyl-CoA. Inhibits CPT-1, blocking fatty acid entry into mitochondria.

MCTs: Medium-chain triglycerides (C6–C12). Bypass CPT-1 and are rapidly oxidized in the liver.

mtDNA: Mitochondrial DNA. A circular, 16.5kb genome encoding 13 ETC subunits. Lacks histones and has limited repair capacity.

PDH: Pyruvate dehydrogenase. Converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA. Requires thiamine (B1) as co-factor. Inhibited by acetyl-CoA and NADH (Randle Cycle).

PGC-1α: Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha. Master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis.

PFK-1: Phosphofructokinase-1. Major rate-limiting enzyme of glycolysis. Inhibited by cytoplasmic citrate.

Randle Cycle: The glucose–fatty acid cycle: reciprocal substrate competition between glucose and fatty acids for oxidation (Randle et al., 1963).

ROS: Reactive oxygen species. Byproducts of ETC electron leak, primarily superoxide (O₂⁻). Manageable in small amounts; damaging in excess.

Resistant starch (RS): Starch that resists α-amylase hydrolysis in the small intestine and reaches the colon for bacterial fermentation.

TMAO: Trimethylamine N-oxide. A metabolite of carnitine/choline produced by gut bacteria; associated with cardiovascular risk in observational studies.

Warburg effect: Aerobic glycolysis: preferential glucose metabolism via glycolysis even in the presence of oxygen. First observed in cancer cells (Warburg, 1924).
