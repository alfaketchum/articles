Key Points About Japan's Economic Situation:

1. Rising Interest Rates

  • February 2025 saw long-term interest rates reaching 1.45%, the highest in 15 years
  • BOJ Governor Ueda tried to intervene to calm the market

2. Bank of Japan's Precarious Position

  • BOJ holds more than half of all Japanese Government Bonds (JGBs)
  • The central bank faces significant valuation losses (estimated at 2.9 trillion yen with just a 0.1% rise in rates)
  • These losses threaten to undermine trust in the central bank

3. Market Liquidity Problems

  • Fujimaki expressed concern about market liquidity, noting "you can't sell when you want to sell"
  • This creates a dangerous situation, especially for "salaried traders" in Japan

4. BOJ's Fiscal Policy Dilemma

  • BOJ has purchased approximately 95% of JGBs since 2013
  • They face a contradictory situation: to fight inflation they should stop buying bonds or raise rates, but doing so would crash bond prices and increase their losses
  • Governor Ueda stated they would increase JGB purchases if rates rise sharply, which Fujimaki called "outrageous" and "feeding oil into the fire" of inflation

5. Interest Rate Imbalance

  • The BOJ is described as making a fundamental error by "raising short-term interest rates but investing long-term"
  • This is compared to the Silicon Valley Bank failure in the US
  • The BOJ was described as "the largest long-term investor" putting itself in a situation where it "can't raise policy rates"

6. Fiscal Finance Concerns

  • Fujimaki noted that "fiscal finance" (central banks financing government spending) is "banned all over the world" because it leads to these kinds of problems
  • Both speakers express concern that Japan has reached its limit in this approach

7. The Yen's Value

  • The yen was touching 148 yen to the dollar at the time of recording
  • Fujimaki advised listeners to protect themselves by "buying dollars" rather than depending on the government and BOJ

8. Analogy Used

Fujimaki compared Japan's situation to discussing "whether it is high tide or low tide" while a tsunami warning has been issued. He warned that financial collapse could "come overnight" and not to be distracted by small market movements.

9. International Perception

  • Watanabe predicted that "the international community will give up on the Bank of Japan" due to Governor Ueda's policies

Both speakers appeared extremely concerned about Japan's financial situation, describing it as a crisis with no clear solution, and suggested that Japan's debt situation has become "radical" with limited options remaining.
