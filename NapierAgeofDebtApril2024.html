Age of Debt China Napier Foward Guidance April 2024

Q1: How do you spot a change in monetary policy and what triggers it?

  • Jack’s Question: "How do you spot a change in monetary policy and what triggers it? Is it falling bond prices, widening credit spreads, or certain equity valuations?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • Structural changes in monetary policy are not simply about cyclically moving interest rates; they involve a fundamental shift in the system.
    • Historical examples:
      • End of the Gold Standard: UK in 1931 and US in 1933, abandoned due to debt deflation and economic contraction.
      • Collapse of Bretton Woods: A system that, by 1971, became too inflationary and unsustainable.
    • Such shifts are rare in an investor’s lifetime, but their impact can last for decades.

Q2: What gave birth to the “Age of Debt” and how has it shaped the global monetary system?

  • Jack’s Question: "Can you review for our listeners what gave birth to the Age of Debt and how would you characterize it over the past three decades?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • The modern monetary regime emerged in 1994 when China devalued its currency and pegged the Yuan at about 8.7 per USD.
    • Key outcomes:
      • Chinese exports became highly competitive due to the artificially cheap Yuan.
      • Massive trade surpluses and capital inflows enabled China to accumulate huge amounts of U.S. Treasuries and other developed-world debt.
      • The system encouraged leverage—cheap debt relative to growth—and boosted global asset prices.
    • Financial Metric:
      • At its peak, China’s current account surplus was around 10% of GDP.

Q3: Was the Yuan artificially cheap in 1994, and what is its status now?

  • Jack’s Question: "Is it correct to say that in 1994 the Yuan was artificially cheap, and how competitive is it now?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • In 1994, the fixed exchange rate (8.7 Yuan per USD) provided an artificially cheap environment that spurred export growth.
    • Today:
      • The current account surplus has collapsed from around 10% of GDP to roughly 1.5% of GDP.
      • Shrinking external surpluses and rising capital outflows indicate that the Yuan appears overvalued under the current fixed regime.
    • This is a key signal that China may be forced to adopt a flexible exchange rate.

Q4: What choice do Chinese monetary authorities face now?

  • Jack’s Question: "Given these conditions, does China face a choice between devaluing the Yuan or enduring a contractionary recession similar to what some Southeast Asian economies experienced?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • China faces a “trilemma”: it cannot simultaneously control the exchange rate, interest rate policy, and the growth of credit.
    • Diminishing external surpluses and mounting capital outflows make maintaining a fixed exchange rate unsustainable.
    • To avoid a deflationary spiral driven by debt overhang, China will likely need to devalue its currency by adopting a flexible exchange rate.
    • Financial Metric:
      • China’s total debt-to-GDP ratio is approximately 311% compared to about 254% in the U.S.

Q5: How do stock market valuations signal underlying monetary issues?

  • Jack’s Question: "You mentioned that when equity valuations (cyclically adjusted P/E ratios) hit around 10x, it indicates dire economic outlooks. Can you explain how this applies to the Chinese stock market?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • Historically, major monetary shifts have coincided with equity markets trading at cyclically adjusted P/E ratios near 10x.
    • Examples include turning points in U.S. history (e.g., 1932, 1949, 1982) where equities were around or below this level.
    • Today, Chinese equities trade at a CAPE of roughly 10x—a level that may signal deflationary pressures and structural risks, despite appearing “cheap.”
    • Russell warns that, given geopolitical tensions and potential capital controls, the true risk for foreign investors is a dramatic devaluation of these equities, potentially even to zero.

Q6: How does Japan’s monetary policy compare, and what might change there?

  • Jack’s Question: "Japanese interest rates remain extremely low with the Bank of Japan using yield curve control. How can the Yen devalue further, and what changes do you foresee?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • Japan’s ultra-low interest rate policy is maintained through massive quantitative easing and yield curve control, leading to an exceptionally cheap long-term rate.
    • The Yen has devalued significantly compared to historical norms due to ongoing QE measures.
    • Looking ahead, Japan may shift to a form of financial repression—compelling domestic savings institutions to purchase Japanese Government Bonds (JGBs) rather than relying solely on central bank asset purchases.
    • This shift aims to manage the exchange rate while curbing the unsustainable expansion of bank reserves.

Q7: What does quantitative easing in Japan teach us about monetary policy?

  • Jack’s Question: "Quantitative easing in Japan led to a huge increase in bank reserves without a corresponding expansion in broad money. What lessons does this offer?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • QE increased commercial bank reserves massively, but this did not translate into proportional growth in broad money (M2).
    • Excess liquidity instead pushed asset prices higher, particularly in equities and bonds.
    • The misalignment between bank balance sheet growth and real economic expansion may force a policy reorientation, such as financial repression.

Q8: How do Chinese companies with low P/E ratios fit into this picture?

  • Jack’s Question: "Many investors tout Chinese companies like Alibaba and JD.com as bargains because of low P/E ratios. How does your analysis explain these valuations given the broader macro risks?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • Headline valuations (e.g., P/E ratios around 5 for some firms) may look attractive but can be misleading.
    • Earnings may be inflated through financial engineering (borrowing cheaply in both local and foreign currencies).
    • Once interest rates are reset to more realistic levels, these artificially boosted earnings could vanish, exposing underlying vulnerabilities.
    • This means that the apparent “margin of safety” might be illusory, particularly given the geopolitical and capital control risks.

Q9: How should investors view devaluation in relation to equity returns?

  • Jack’s Question: "If a currency devalues, like what happens to a British investor in Japanese stocks or a U.S. investor in Chinese stocks, how do we balance the gains in local currency terms with the depreciation risk?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • A devaluation could boost the dollar-denominated value of equities if the rise in equity valuations outpaces the currency loss.
    • For instance, if Chinese equity valuations rise from a CAPE of 10 to levels comparable with the U.K. or U.S. (say 32), that increase (potentially 40–50%) could more than offset the devaluation percentage.
    • However, if corporate balance sheets suffer permanent damage (e.g., due to unhedged foreign currency debt), the net effect might be negative.
    • Institutional investors may hedge some currency risks, but the underlying macro uncertainty remains.

Q10: Are high government interest rates inflationary or deflationary?

  • Jack’s Question: "Do you think that high government interest rates end up being inflationary because they force governments to print more money, or do they instead trigger deflationary reforms?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • The answer isn’t straightforward. In many cases, governments choose inflation over austerity to manage unsustainable debt levels.
    • Rising rates tend to force changes in fiscal behavior—either by cutting spending or by monetizing debt, which can lead to inflation.
    • Ultimately, worsening government debt burdens are likely to result in higher inflation as policymakers attempt to “inflate away” the debt.

Q11: How momentous is the impending monetary shift compared to historical changes like the end of Bretton Woods?

  • Jack’s Question: "How do you compare the scale of the coming monetary change—especially in Asia—with historical shifts such as the end of the gold standard or the collapse of Bretton Woods?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • While not as dramatic as abandoning the gold standard, the upcoming shift is significant.
    • It will likely force governments to reallocate private savings into government bonds—a hallmark of financial repression.
    • This new regime may result in persistently higher inflation as governments try to reduce real debt burdens.
    • Though the change may not replicate exact historical events, it will fundamentally alter the management of monetary policy and global capital flows.

Q12: What should investors be paying attention to as these changes unfold?

  • Jack’s Final Question: "What do you think investors in the financial world are not paying attention to but should, given these impending structural shifts?"
  • Russell’s Response:
    • Investors often focus solely on traditional market metrics and assume free-market dynamics will persist.
    • Key areas to watch include:
      • The growing divergence between debt levels and economic growth. For example, China’s debt-to-GDP is around 311% compared to the U.S. at 254%.
      • Shifts in monetary policy that could trigger dramatic moves in exchange rates and asset valuations.
      • The potential for governments to impose capital controls and engage in financial repression to manage unsustainable debt.
    • Russell emphasizes that the most undervalued factor may be the possibility of the current free-market system being replaced by one where state intervention redefines asset allocation.
